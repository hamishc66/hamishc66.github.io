01 / WHY

Why I Did It

End of my first year at OSU, studying emergency management. I'd been reading about the backcountry — terrain, weather patterns, search and rescue operations — and at some point I noticed the gap between what I was reading and what I'd actually done. Weekend trips don't close that gap. You get uncomfortable for 48 hours and then you go home and get a burrito.

I wanted to find out if I could handle sustained hardship. Not "hard weekend" hard — months hard. The kind where you can't just tap out. Where the question of whether you're cut out for this gets answered whether you want it answered or not. That's a different thing than a certification exam or a class scenario.

There was also a practical angle. Emergency management in the West means working in terrain that runs from high desert to alpine. I wanted to understand that terrain in my body, not just on a map. The PCT covers all of it. You start at the Mexican border with 26 pounds on your back and a lot of that kind of understanding comes pretty fast.

April 2031. Campo, California. It was hot already.

02 / THE TRAIL

What It Was Actually Like

I'm going to be straight with you: the first two weeks were rough. Southern California chaparral in April means dust, rattlesnakes, and temperatures that swing 40 degrees between 2 AM and 2 PM. I had blisters on my blisters. My pack was too heavy. I was hiking 18 miles a day instead of the 25 I'd planned. There's a specific kind of humility that hits you around mile 80 when you realize you've got 2,570 to go and your feet look like something from a medical textbook.

But you figure it out. You shed gear. You learn the rhythm — water sources, shade, when to push and when to set up camp early. By the time I hit the San Bernardino Mountains, something had clicked.

Segment 01
The Desert — Southern California

More brutal than anyone tells you. Endless miles of chaparral and sand, no shade, water every 20+ miles sometimes. The learning curve is steep and the desert doesn't care. You either adapt your pace and your head, or you quit. A lot of people quit here.

Segment 02
The Sierra Nevada

The most technically demanding stretch. I hit the High Sierra in late May — still plenty of snow on the passes. Icy approaches at dawn, route-finding with the trail buried under three feet of white, post-holing through cornices. This is where the navigation skills got real. No shortcuts up here.

Segment 03
Oregon

Volcanic terrain, crater lakes, old-growth forest. Physically, Oregon is the recovery section — flatter, faster miles. Mentally, it's a trap. After the Sierra, you let your guard down. The miles blur together. You cruise on autopilot and suddenly it's September and you're crossing into Washington.

Segment 04
Washington — The North Cascades

Relentless rain. Mud. Everything soaked all the time. The Cascades are genuinely beautiful — volcanic peaks, old-growth hemlock, the Pacific mist — but you're grinding through it on a body that's been walking for five months. The last week felt almost anticlimactic. You're just running on routine by then.

Trail culture is its own thing. You get a trail name — mine was "Cairns," which was the obvious call for someone named Cairns, and also because I kept building little rock cairns at confusing junctions. People start knowing you by that name. You share a camp with someone for one night and you know their real deal by morning — there's not much pretense when everyone's dirty and tired and eating ramen.

Trail magic is real. Strangers leaving coolers of cold Cokes at road crossings. A guy in Idyllwild who turned his backyard into an unofficial hostel every summer, no charge. Random acts of generosity from people who have nothing to gain from it. That stuff lands differently when you're exhausted and it's hot and you've been going on willpower for a week.

The hard parts no one posts about: Loneliness in the desert, especially the first few hundred miles before the hiker community consolidates. Days when the miles don't add up and you're setting up your tent in the dark, again, in the rain, eating cold food because your fuel ran out. Heat exhaustion around mile 300 — I stopped too late in the day and paid for it. The trail tests your decision-making every single day, often when you're the most tired and the most hungry, which is exactly when your judgment is the worst.

"The trail doesn't care about your resume. It cares if you can walk tomorrow."

— somewhere around mile 1,400, northern California
03 / WHAT IT BUILT

What It Actually Built

I want to be clear about what I'm not claiming: hiking a long trail doesn't make you a paramedic. It doesn't replace clinical training or incident command coursework. What it does is build the substrate that the technical skills sit on. And that substrate is hard to fake.

  • Decision-Making Under Fatigue

    On trail, the most important decisions happen when you're exhausted, hungry, and out of water. Do you push another 4 miles to the next reliable source or camp dry? Do you attempt that snow pass in the afternoon when it's softening, or bivouac and go at dawn? Those aren't abstract calls. Emergency work asks the exact same thing of you — best judgment under the worst conditions. Six months of that is a decent training ground.

  • Self-Rescue Mentality

    Nobody's coming. That's the baseline assumption in the Sierra, especially early-season, especially off the main corridor. You take a bad fall, you twist an ankle on a snow crossing — you figure it out with what you have. That mindset shift — away from "call for help" toward "what do I have and what do I do with it right now" — is the foundation of wilderness medicine. It's also just cleaner thinking under stress.

  • Navigation Without a Signal

    Six months of reading terrain, crosschecking paper maps with GPS, noting water sources, tracking weather, identifying landmarks at a distance. There's no cell service across most of the Sierra and half of Washington. You get very comfortable very fast with map-and-compass as the primary system, technology as the backup. That hierarchy matters in the field.

  • Reading People

    You spend enough time in the hiking community and you learn to tell the difference between someone who says they're fine and someone who actually is. The vibe, the eyes, how they're moving — you pick up on it fast because the stakes are real. Someone bonking from heat exhaustion looks different than someone who's just tired. That read transfers directly to field triage and patient assessment.

  • Comfort With Discomfort

    This one's harder to quantify but it might be the most valuable thing. I'm genuinely comfortable being wet, cold, hungry, and tired for extended periods — not because I enjoy it, but because I know it ends, I know how to manage it, and I know it doesn't impair my ability to function if I don't let it. That's something no cert program gives you. It has to be earned.

  • Patience and Stubbornness

    Twenty-five miles a day for months isn't complicated. One foot in front of the other, every day, no days off. That builds a specific kind of patient stubbornness that's really hard to fake in an interview but pretty obvious when something goes sideways in the field. You either have the gear to grind, or you don't. Trail sorts that out pretty efficiently.
