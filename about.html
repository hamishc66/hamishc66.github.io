01 / WHO HE IS

Where He's From

I grew up in Oregon — the Willamette Valley, mostly. Not the city part. I spent more time in the woods than my parents probably intended, which I don't think they actually minded once it became clear I was coming back each time. I was the kind of kid who went camping with the scouts and kept going long after the scouts stopped being relevant. By high school the gear was better and the trips were longer, but the impulse was the same: get into the trees, stay out as long as possible, come back knowing something you didn't know before.

I've always wanted to be useful in a crisis. Not heroic — that's a different thing entirely and usually a warning sign. Just useful. The guy who actually knows what to do when things go sideways in the middle of nowhere, when the cell signal is gone and the nearest road is twelve miles behind you. That's the version of competence I've been chasing.

I have a complicated relationship with cities. I respect them, I understand why most people live in them, I just don't particularly want to. Oregon's wilderness is too close and too good. The coast range, the Cascades, the high desert — it's all right there. Trading any of that for a shorter commute doesn't make sense to me yet. Maybe someday the calculus shifts. Right now it hasn't.

The outdoors isn't a hobby for me. It's the operating environment. Everything I've learned — medicine, navigation, communications, logistics — has been oriented toward being competent in places where mistakes have real consequences and where you can't call someone else to sort it out. That framing shapes how I approach almost everything.

02 / HOW HE GOT HERE

The Path

I started at OSU pursuing Natural Resources. I wanted to understand the land — the ecology, the systems, the disasters that reshape it. Wildfires, floods, landslides. The way landscapes change and how human response fits into that. It felt like the honest version of what I actually cared about, versus something I was doing because it seemed practical.

My second year I joined ALI — the Adventure Leadership Institute. That's where I got the technical foundation. Rope rescue, swiftwater, technical terrain management. People who knew what they were doing and expected you to learn fast. I did. That program did more for my actual field competence than anything else I've been part of.

Between sophomore and junior year I did the PCT. Six months, 2,650 miles, Campo to Manning Park. I came back different — not in some mystical way, just more settled in what I wanted to do. There's something about six months of sustained forward motion through genuine wilderness that clarifies things. You find out what matters to you when you're too tired to maintain any pretense about it.

"You find out what matters to you when you're too tired to maintain any pretense about it. Six months on trail has a way of settling the question."

— On the PCT

After the trail I started stacking certifications in a way that felt more purposeful. WFA first, then WFR, then EMT-B, then EMT-A. HAM radio along the way. Each one made the next one make more sense — the skills compound in a way that isn't obvious until you're actually using them. The WFR gave me the clinical framework; the EMT training sharpened it into something operational.

I also picked up the GIS track within my Natural Resources degree. That one wasn't planned — I took a spatial analysis course mostly out of curiosity and realized it was a genuine force multiplier for everything else I was doing. Knowing where things are and why changes what's possible during a response. It changed how I thought about the whole field.

Now I'm working as an emergency management contractor with Oregon State Parks while finishing the degree. Real incidents, real coordination, real stakes. The classroom version and the field version don't always match up and I prefer knowing which one to trust.

03 / WHAT HE BELIEVES

The Philosophy

"I don't do this for the adrenaline. I do it because someone has to be the person who shows up when things go wrong in places where help is far away. I'd rather it be someone who's prepared."

Emergency response in the backcountry is mostly boring. I want to be clear about that. It's logistics, preparation, communication, and waiting. It's maintaining gear you hope you never use and practicing scenarios that may never come up. The exciting moment — the actual rescue, the actual patient — is usually a signal that something went wrong earlier in the planning process. Good planning is quiet. The drama comes from gaps in the planning.

The best response is the one that didn't need to happen. Good prevention, good communication, well-briefed groups that know how to self-rescue. When the response does happen, though, you want someone who's done the preparation. The preparation isn't glamorous and it never makes the after-action report. It just shapes outcomes.

Certifications matter. I know that can sound defensive, like I'm justifying the time spent. But they're not just paper — they represent hours of supervised practice and pattern recognition that shows up when you're stressed and your brain is looking for shortcuts. The trained response and the instinctive response converge. That convergence doesn't happen without repetition under real conditions, and the certifications are how the field structures that repetition.

I think the wilderness medicine community often has more practical emergency response knowledge than people realize. It's compressed, unglamorous, and relentlessly oriented toward real outcomes rather than theoretical ones. Hospital medicine has resources that change what's possible. Wilderness medicine has to work with what's in the pack and what's in the environment. That constraint makes it interesting and it makes the practitioners surprisingly capable in degraded conditions.

04 / THE PERSONAL STUFF

Off the Clock

I still hike. Not always for training — sometimes just to walk. Oregon's trail system is extraordinary and most people genuinely don't use it. The state has more miles of quality trail per capita than almost anywhere in the country and a significant chunk of the population has never been on most of it. I don't fully understand this but I try not to be too judgmental about it.

I climb when I can, mostly out of Smith Rock. Some routes in the Sisters area when the season allows. Rock climbing and wilderness medicine share a certain quality of focus — the problem is immediate, the consequences of inattention are clear, and there's no way to fake competence for very long. I find that combination useful for staying honest about what I actually know versus what I think I know.

I work on my F-150. This is probably the thing people are most surprised by. I have what I'll admit is an unreasonable attachment to keeping old vehicles running, and there's something genuinely satisfying about understanding a machine well enough to fix it yourself. Not just replace parts — actually diagnose and repair. The truck is also a field asset, which gives me an operational justification for the hobby, but honestly I'd probably do it anyway.

"There's something satisfying about understanding a machine well enough to fix it yourself. The truck is an extension of the operation — power, comms, gear — and a broken-down truck is a cancelled mission."

— On field mobility

I read a lot of history and military operations — less for the tactics than for the logistics. The tactics part gets most of the attention in popular history but the logistics are where the actual decisions live. Understanding how organizations function under pressure, how supply chains break down, how communication degrades in dynamic environments — that stuff is interesting regardless of context and it directly informs how I think about incident management.

I cook my own food in the backcountry. Real food, not just freeze-dried packets, though I'm not opposed to those when the situation calls for it. I fundamentally disagree with the ultralight community on this point and I'm comfortable saying so. The caloric density argument is real. The morale argument is also real. A good meal at camp after a long day matters in ways that pure efficiency metrics don't capture. I carry the extra weight and I don't apologize for it.

05 / WHAT'S NEXT

The Forward Look

Finishing the degree in 2035. That's the immediate logistical fact. The degree matters not because I think it changes my field capabilities dramatically at this point, but because it opens certain institutional doors and provides a formal framework for the GIS work that I want to develop further. It's also just the right thing to finish.

After that I want to work in emergency management at a scale where the spatial intelligence skills actually matter — regional SAR coordination, wildfire support, disaster response. The kind of work where someone with a real GIS background and field experience is genuinely rare rather than just useful. The intersection of those two things is where I want to be operating.

I'm specifically interested in how spatial data changes decision-making during active incidents. Real-time GIS in incident management is still maturing as a field. Most operations are working with data that's hours or days old. The infrastructure exists now to do better than that and the bottleneck is people who understand both the data systems and the operational context. I want to be one of those people.

Long-term: probably running a SAR operation or working with a federal land management agency in a role that keeps me in the field rather than in an office. The administrative side of emergency management is real and important and I'll do it when it needs to be done. But the goal is to stay connected to the actual work — the terrain, the operations, the decisions that matter in the moment.

I'd teach wilderness medicine eventually. That feels like a natural endpoint for the certifications and the experience, not because teaching is a retirement activity but because passing practical skills to people who will actually use them is one of the more genuinely useful things a person can do. The wilderness medicine community is only as good as its instructors. I'd like to contribute to that.
